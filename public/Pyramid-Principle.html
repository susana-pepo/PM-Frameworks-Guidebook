🔺 Pyramid Principle

Answer first, then support. Structure any message so the most important point comes first, with grouped evidence underneath. Created by Barbara Minto at McKinsey.

⚡

One-liner: Start with your conclusion/recommendation, then group your supporting arguments into 2-4 buckets, each with its own evidence. The audience gets the answer immediately and can dig into details if they want.

Why Top-Down?

How executives process information

The Problem: Bottom-Up Communication

Most people communicate the way they think: "I researched X, then analyzed Y, then compared Z, and my conclusion is..." This is bottom-up. The audience has to wait until the end to know what you're recommending.

Executives don't have time for that. They want: "Here's what I recommend. Here's why. Here's the evidence." If they're convinced after the recommendation, they stop. If they want detail, they go deeper. This is top-down communication.

❌ Bottom-Up (Bad)
"We looked at user data. Then we ran surveys. Then we benchmarked competitors. Then we analyzed costs. So we think we should maybe consider investing in mobile onboarding."
Executive tuned out at sentence 2.
✅ Top-Down (Pyramid)
"We should invest in mobile onboarding. Three reasons: 60% of our users are mobile-only, mobile converts 2x higher than web, and all three competitors launched it this quarter. Here's the data..."
Executive has the answer + reasons in 10 seconds.

🧠 Memory Trick

Think of a newspaper article. The headline is the conclusion. The first paragraph summarizes why. The rest gives detail. You can stop reading at any point and still have the key message. That's pyramid structure.

Minto's 3 Rules

How to build a pyramid

Rule 1: Start with the Answer

Your recommendation or conclusion goes first. Not at the end. Not buried in a paragraph. First sentence. If the reader only reads one sentence, they get the key message.

Rule 2: Group & Summarize

Support your answer with 2-4 grouped arguments. Each group should be MECE (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive) — no overlaps, no gaps. Each group gets its own summary sentence.

Group by time: Past → Present → Future
Group by structure: Problem → Solution → Impact
Group by priority: Most → Least important
Rule 3: Logically Order Within Groups

Within each group, arrange evidence in logical order: deductive (general → specific), inductive (specific observations → conclusion), or chronological. Each sub-point must directly support its group's summary.

💡 MECE Explained

Mutually Exclusive: No argument overlaps with another. If "User data" and "Survey results" cover the same insight, merge them. Collectively Exhaustive: Together, your arguments fully support the conclusion. If someone asks "but what about X?" and X is a major consideration you missed, your groups aren't exhaustive.

SCQA Introduction

How to open your pyramid

The Setup Before the Answer

Before your main answer, use SCQA to set up WHY this matters. It creates a question in the reader's mind that your answer resolves.

Situation — Shared context everyone agrees on. "Skoove has 5M users and 70% churn in week 1."
Complication — What changed or what's wrong. "Despite increasing ad spend 40%, acquisition hasn't improved retention."
Question — The question this raises. "How do we improve retention without increasing acquisition cost?"
Answer — Your recommendation. "Invest in mobile onboarding redesign." ← This is the top of your pyramid.

🔗 SCQA + Pyramid = Complete Structure

SCQA opens the communication (creates the need). Pyramid delivers the answer (top-down with grouped evidence). Together: Situation → Complication → Question → Answer → Reason 1 → Reason 2 → Reason 3 → Evidence.

Real Example

Pyramid for a Skoove stakeholder update

🎹 Slack Message to CPO: "Q3 Content Strategy Proposal"

S: Our D7 retention is 12%, well below the 20% benchmark for music ed apps.
C: Analysis shows 80% of drop-offs happen during lessons 3-5, where difficulty jumps sharply.
Q: How do we smooth the difficulty curve without dumbing down the product?
🎯 A: I recommend we launch "bridge lessons" — short review exercises between difficulty jumps — targeting a 10pp retention improvement by end of Q3.

Supporting Pyramid:

Reason 1 — User data supports it: Lesson replay rate is 4x higher for lessons 3-5 vs others. Users WANT to review but there's no structured way to do it.
Reason 2 — Low effort, high impact: Bridge lessons reuse existing content (exercises from prior lessons). Engineering estimate: 2 sprints. Content creation: 1 week per bridge set.
Reason 3 — Competitor validation: Simply Piano launched "review sessions" in Q1 and reported 15% retention improvement in their earnings call.

💡 Why This Works

The CPO gets the recommendation in 10 seconds. If convinced, they approve immediately. If skeptical, they read the 3 reasons. If they want detail, they ask. You've respected their time while being thorough. Compare this to a 2-page analysis that buries the recommendation at the end.

When to Use

Pyramid fits almost everywhere

✅ Use Pyramid When...

  • ✔️ Stakeholder updates and executive emails
  • ✔️ PRDs and product briefs
  • ✔️ Slack messages that need a decision
  • ✔️ Case interview answers (structure your analysis)
  • ✔️ Meeting agendas and kick-off docs
  • ✔️ Any time you're asking someone for a decision

⛔ Skip Pyramid When...

  • ❌ Behavioral interviews (use STAR instead)
  • ❌ Brainstorming — you want divergent thinking, not conclusions
  • ❌ Emotional/sensitive messages — leading with conclusion can feel cold
  • ❌ When the audience needs to discover the answer themselves

🔗 Connections

STAR
STAR = tell a story (interviews). Pyramid = deliver a conclusion (stakeholder comms). Different goals, both structured.
Exec Summary
Executive Summary IS a pyramid in document form. The summary = answer. Sections = grouped reasons.
Narrative Arc
Narrative Arc builds tension before the answer. Pyramid skips tension and leads with the answer. Choose based on audience.
