✏️ Design Framework

Design Thinking

A human-centered process for solving problems people actually have.

💜Empathize
🎯Define
💡Ideate
🔨Prototype
🧪Test
⚡

In 15 seconds: Design Thinking is a 5-phase process from Stanford's d.school. Start with understanding the human (Empathize), frame the real problem (Define), generate many ideas (Ideate), make it tangible (Prototype), and learn from reality (Test). It's iterative — you loop back constantly.

🔄

The 5 Phases

Not linear — you loop back constantly

🧠 UNDERSTAND THE PROBLEM
💜
Empathize

Observe and talk to real users. Understand their world, feelings, and frustrations. No assumptions — just listen.

🎯
Define

Synthesize what you learned into a clear problem statement. Frame: "[User] needs [need] because [insight]."

✨ EXPLORE SOLUTIONS
💡
Ideate

Generate as many ideas as possible. Quantity over quality. Build on others' ideas. No judgment yet — wild ideas welcome.

🚀 MATERIALIZE & LEARN
🔨
Prototype

Make the idea tangible. Doesn't need to be perfect — paper, wireframes, mockups. Fast and cheap.

🧪
Test

Put the prototype in front of users. Watch what happens. Listen. Learn. Then loop back to refine.

🔄 It's not a waterfall

The arrows go both ways. Testing might send you back to Empathize. Defining the problem might show you need more research. That's not failure — that's the process working. Each loop makes the solution better.

🧠 Memory trick: the Double Diamond

Design Thinking follows a diverge → converge pattern twice. First diamond: go wide exploring the problem (Empathize), then narrow to one problem (Define). Second diamond: go wide with solutions (Ideate), then narrow to one prototype (Prototype → Test).

↗ Diverge (Empathize)
↘ Converge (Define)
↗ Diverge (Ideate)
↘ Converge (Proto+Test)
🔍

Each Phase in Detail

What to do, how long, what comes out

⏱ 1–2 weeks

1
Empathize

"What is the user's actual lived experience?"

Immerse yourself in the user's world. Don't ask what they want — watch what they do. The gap between what people say and what they actually do is where insights live.

🛠 Methods: User interviews (5–8 people) · Contextual observation (watch them in their environment) · Journey mapping · Empathy mapping (says, thinks, does, feels) · Shadowing
Empathy maps, interview transcripts, observation notes, quotes, photos/videos
⏱ 2–4 hours

2
Define

"What is the REAL problem we're solving?"

Synthesize research into one clear problem statement. The format: "[User] needs [need] because [surprising insight]." This is called a Point of View (POV) statement. Getting this right is the hardest and most important step.

🛠 Methods: Affinity mapping (cluster sticky notes into themes) · "How Might We" questions · POV statement writing · Insight synthesis
1 clear POV statement + 3–5 "How Might We" questions to guide ideation
⏱ 1–2 hours

3
Ideate

"How many ways could we solve this?"

Generate as many ideas as possible. Rules: no criticism, wild ideas welcome, build on others' ideas, go for quantity. The goal is 50–100 ideas, not 5 "good" ones. The best ideas often come from combining unexpected concepts.

🛠 Methods: Brainstorming (timed, silent-first) · Crazy 8s (8 sketches in 8 minutes) · SCAMPER · Mind mapping · Worst possible idea (then flip it)
50–100 raw ideas → dot-voted down to 3–5 top concepts
⏱ 1–3 days

4
Prototype

"How do we make it real enough to learn from?"

Build the cheapest, fastest version that lets you test the core assumption. A prototype is a question made tangible. It should take hours, not weeks. If it's not embarrassingly rough, you spent too long on it.

🛠 Methods: Paper prototypes · Figma clickable mockups · Wizard of Oz (fake the backend) · Storyboards · Landing page test · Role-playing
A testable artifact that embodies 1–2 key assumptions
⏱ 1–2 weeks

5
Test

"Does this actually solve the problem for real users?"

Put the prototype in front of 5–8 users. Don't explain how it works — watch if they can figure it out. Listen more than talk. Your job is to learn, not to sell. Every test generates insights that feed the next loop.

🛠 Methods: Usability testing (5 users finds 85% of issues) · A/B testing · Feedback interviews · Task completion tracking · Think-aloud protocol
Validated/invalidated assumptions → refined problem statement or improved prototype → loop back
The phases aren't equal in time. Empathize and Test are the longest. Define is the shortest but hardest. Ideate should be fast and energetic. Prototype should be scrappy.

Full Walkthrough

"Redesign the onboarding for a music learning app"

🎤 Challenge: New users of a music learning app drop off within the first week. Redesign the onboarding experience.

1
Empathize

Interviewed 8 users who signed up but stopped within 7 days. Watched 5 screen recordings of first sessions. Ran an empathy mapping workshop with the team.
Users aren't lazy — they're overwhelmed. The app dumps 200+ lessons on them with no guidance. They don't know where to start, feel like they're "doing it wrong," and give up. The emotional job is "feel like I'm making progress" — and the app fails at this on day 1.

2
Define

POV Statement: "Adult beginner musicians need to feel a sense of progress in their first 10 minutes because the initial overwhelm makes them believe they can't learn, and they quit before they've actually tried."
HMW questions: How might we make the first session feel like a win? How might we reduce choice paralysis? How might we create a "hook moment" in under 5 minutes?

3
Ideate

Crazy 8s session with the team. 67 ideas generated. Dot-voted to top 3: (A) "Play your first song in 5 minutes" guided flow, (B) Personalized path quiz — 3 questions → tailored curriculum, (C) Social proof onboarding — show other beginners' day-1 recordings.
Concept A won the vote. The fastest path to the "I did it!" moment. Combined with a light version of B (one question: "What song do you want to play?").

4
Prototype

Built a Figma clickable prototype in 2 days. 6 screens: Welcome → "Pick a song" (3 beginner-friendly options) → Simplified lesson with only 4 notes → Play along with real backing track → 🎉 Celebration screen with recording playback → "Keep going?" prompt.
Prototype took 6 hours to build. The celebration screen shows "You just played your first song in 4 minutes!" with confetti animation.

5
Test

Tested with 6 new users in moderated sessions. 5/6 completed the first song. All 6 smiled at the celebration screen. 4/6 immediately tapped "Keep going." Main feedback: "I didn't know it could be this easy."
What we learned: The "pick a song" step needs more options. One user couldn't find a song they knew. The 4-note simplification worked perfectly — it removed the fear of failure. Loop back: add more song options and test again with 5 more users.

🔍 Why this example works

Notice how each phase builds on the last. Empathy revealed the real problem (overwhelm, not content quality). Define turned it into a clear target. Ideate explored broadly. Prototype made it testable in days, not months. Test proved the concept and revealed what to refine next.

Apply to Your Problem

Work through all 5 phases on something real

🎯 Pick a real problem you're facing at work — or use: "Users aren't completing their first lesson"
Think about real users you've talked to. Direct quotes are gold.
The insight should be something non-obvious. If everyone already knows it, dig deeper.
Set a 5-minute timer and write without stopping. Quantity over quality.
The question your prototype answers: "Will users [expected behavior]?"
Define your success criteria BEFORE testing. "If 4/5 users do X, we proceed."
Using a real problem from your work makes this 10x more valuable than a hypothetical. If you're at Trala/Skoove, try it with an onboarding, retention, or engagement challenge.

When to Use Design Thinking

Not for every problem — for the right problems

✅ Use Design Thinking when...

  • You're not sure what the real problem is yet
  • Building something new for an audience you don't fully understand
  • Existing solutions aren't working and you don't know why
  • Stakeholders disagree about what to build — you need shared understanding
  • PM interview asks "How would you approach improving X?"
  • You want to build team empathy for users

🚫 Skip Design Thinking when...

  • The problem is well-understood and you need execution speed
  • It's a bug fix or technical debt — just fix it
  • You need to prioritize known features → use RICE/ICE
  • Stakeholder already mandated the solution (politics, not process)
  • You're optimizing a working flow — A/B test instead
  • You don't have access to real users

🔗 How Design Thinking connects to other frameworks

DT → JTBDEmpathize phase IS JTBD research. Use "When I... I want to... So I can..." to synthesize what you learn from user interviews.
DT → CIRCLESDesign Thinking is the full process. CIRCLES is a compressed interview-friendly version. In real work, use DT. In interviews, use CIRCLES.
DT → RICE/ICEDesign Thinking generates validated ideas. RICE/ICE ranks them for the roadmap. DT is discovery, RICE is delivery planning.
DT → KanoDT reveals what matters. Kano categorizes those needs (Must-Have vs Delighter). Use them in sequence for product strategy.

6 Common Pitfalls

Where most teams fail

1

Skipping Empathize ("We already know our users")

The most dangerous assumption. Teams skip straight to Ideate based on internal opinions. The result: beautifully designed solutions to problems nobody has.

Talk to 5 real users before ANY ideation. Even 30-minute calls change everything. "We already know" is usually "we assume."
2

Vague problem statement

"Users need a better experience" is not a problem statement. If Define is fuzzy, everything downstream is a guess.

Use the template: "[Specific user] needs [specific need] because [surprising insight from research]." If you can't fill in the "because," you haven't done enough empathy work.
3

Judging ideas too early in Ideate

"That won't work because..." kills creativity instantly. When people fear judgment, they only suggest safe, boring ideas.

Enforce "Yes, and..." for the first 20 minutes. Write ideas silently on sticky notes before discussing. Separate idea generation from idea evaluation.
4

Over-investing in the prototype

Spending 3 weeks building a pixel-perfect Figma prototype. Now nobody wants to throw it away when testing reveals problems.

Set a time limit: prototype in 1–2 days max. Paper sketches are fine. The rougher it looks, the more honest feedback you'll get.
5

Testing to confirm, not to learn

"See? They loved it!" Confirmation bias in testing. You ask leading questions, ignore negative signals, and declare success prematurely.

Write your test questions before the session. Ask "show me how you would..." not "do you like this?" Watch behavior, not just words.
6

Treating it as linear (never looping back)

Going through all 5 steps once, delivering the output, done. Real Design Thinking is messy and iterative.

Budget for at least 2 loops. After Test, always ask: "What did we learn that changes our Define statement?" If it changed, loop back.

Running a Design Thinking Workshop

Full-day or half-day format

📋 Before the Workshop

Pre-work (1–2 weeks before): Empathize phase happens BEFORE the workshop. Do user interviews, collect data, prepare empathy maps. The workshop starts at Define, not Empathize. If you try to do all 5 phases in one day, you'll rush the most important parts.

Invite 4–8 people. Mix PMs, designers, engineers, and one customer-facing person. More than 8 = chaos. Fewer than 4 = echo chamber.
0–20
min

💜 Share Empathy Research

Present user quotes, empathy maps, key observations. Everyone reads silently for 5 min, then discuss. Goal: shared understanding of the user's world.

20–50
min

🎯 Define: Affinity Mapping

Everyone writes observations on sticky notes (1 per note). Cluster into themes silently. Name the clusters. Write 3–5 "How Might We" questions. Vote on the most impactful HMW.

50–60
min

🎯 Define: POV Statement

Write the POV together: "[User] needs [need] because [insight]." Debate until you have ONE statement everyone agrees on. This is your North Star for the rest of the workshop.

60–70
min

☕ Break

Let it marinate. People often have their best ideas during breaks.

70–85
min

💡 Ideate: Silent Brainstorm

Everyone writes ideas on sticky notes silently (1 idea per note). Set a timer: 10 minutes. Aim for 8–10 ideas per person. Sticky notes go on the wall.

85–100
min

💡 Ideate: Share & Build

Quick round: each person presents their top 3 ideas (30 sec each). Others add "Yes, and..." builds. Cluster similar ideas.

100–110
min

💡 Ideate: Dot Vote

Everyone gets 3 dots. Vote on ideas (can stack). Top 2–3 ideas move to prototyping. Tie-break: PM decides.

110–170
min

🔨 Prototype: Build in Pairs

Split into pairs. Each pair builds a rough prototype of one winning idea. Paper, whiteboard, Figma — whatever is fastest. 60 min max. Must be testable (someone can interact with it).

170–190
min

🧪 Test: Internal Demo

Each pair presents their prototype. Others role-play as the target user. Give feedback using "I like / I wish / What if" format. Identify next steps for real user testing.

190–200
min

📋 Wrap-Up & Next Steps

Capture: What's our POV? Which prototype moves forward? Who tests with real users? When? Assign owners and deadlines.

⏱ Total: ~3.5 hours

This is the compressed version. A full-day workshop (6–7 hours) gives more time for Ideate and Prototype. The real user testing happens AFTER the workshop — schedule it within 1 week while momentum is high.

Quick Quiz

7 questions to lock it in

1. What are the 5 phases of Design Thinking in order?

Empathize → Define → Ideate → Prototype → Test. And it's iterative — you loop back constantly.

2. What's the output of the Define phase?

The Point of View statement frames the exact problem you're solving. It's the single most important output of the process.

3. In the Ideate phase, should you aim for...

Quantity over quality in Ideate. Wild ideas welcome. The best solutions often come from combining unexpected concepts. Narrow down AFTER generating.

4. How long should a prototype take to build?

If it's not embarrassingly rough, you spent too long. Prototypes are questions made tangible — they exist to learn, not to impress.

5. What's the Double Diamond?

First diamond: go wide (Empathize), then narrow (Define). Second diamond: go wide (Ideate), then narrow (Prototype + Test). The shape helps you remember the rhythm.

6. The most common and dangerous pitfall is...

Skipping empathy work means you're solving assumed problems, not real ones. "We already know" is the most expensive assumption in product development.

7. How does Design Thinking relate to CIRCLES?

CIRCLES maps roughly: Comprehend ≈ Empathize, Identify/Report ≈ Define, List ≈ Ideate, Evaluate ≈ Prototype/Test, Summarize ≈ present your recommendation. In real work use DT, in interviews use CIRCLES.
