The Core Idea

Why strategy needs a map

The Problem with Strategy Without Maps

Imagine a general planning a battle without a map. They know the troops (capabilities) and the goal (win), but they can't see the terrain, where the enemy is, or how things are changing. That's how most companies do strategy — they plan without situational awareness.

Simon Wardley created Wardley Mapping to give strategy the equivalent of a geographical map. It shows: what you need, how visible it is to users, how evolved it is, and crucially — where things are heading.

🧭
Situational Awareness

See the full landscape — your value chain, dependencies, and how evolved each component is.

📈
Everything Evolves

Every component moves from novel → custom → product → commodity over time. This movement is predictable.

⚙️
Build, Buy, or Outsource

Position on the map tells you: build novel things, buy products, outsource commodities.

🧠 Memory Trick

Think of it as Google Maps for your product strategy. Y-axis = how close to the user (like zoom level). X-axis = how mature (like road quality — dirt path on the left, highway on the right). Components that are still on dirt paths need different treatment than ones on highways.

Anatomy of a Wardley Map

Two axes, components, and dependencies

The Two Axes

↕️ Y-Axis: Value Chain (Visibility)
Top: What the user directly sees and cares about (the app, the UI).
Bottom: Invisible infrastructure (cloud, databases, SDKs).
↔️ X-Axis: Evolution
Left: Novel, uncertain, needs exploration.
Right: Commodity, well-understood, just buy/rent it.

🔧 5 Steps to Build a Map

1
Start with the user need
What does the user ultimately want? This goes at the top of the map.
2
List components needed to serve that need
What do you need to deliver? App, content, technology, infrastructure, data, people...
3
Draw dependencies
What depends on what? Draw lines connecting components. User need depends on app, app depends on content + tech, etc.
4
Place each component on the evolution axis
Is it novel (genesis), emerging (custom), mature (product), or a utility (commodity)?
5
Add movement arrows
Everything moves right over time. Mark which components are actively evolving. This is where strategic decisions live.
💡 First map tip: Don't try to be perfect. A rough map that reveals one insight is infinitely better than no map. You can refine later.
📈

The Evolution Axis

Everything moves from genesis to commodity

The most powerful idea in Wardley Mapping: every component evolves in the same direction, from novel and uncertain to commodity and standard. This is predictable. And it tells you how to treat each component.

I. Genesis
• Brand new, never done before
• Uncertain, experimental
• High failure rate
• Needs agile, exploration mindset
Action: EXPLORE. Build in-house. Tolerate failure.
II. Custom-Built
• Proven concept, bespoke implementation
• Few providers, expensive
• Competitive advantage if done well
• Needs skilled engineers
Action: BUILD carefully. This is your differentiator.
III. Product (+Rental)
• Multiple providers, features converging
• "Good enough" solutions exist
• Decreasing competitive advantage
• Buy vs. build decision point
Action: BUY or adopt existing solutions. Stop custom-building.
IV. Commodity / Utility
• Standardized, interchangeable
• Price is the only differentiator
• Zero competitive advantage
• "Just works" expectation
Action: OUTSOURCE. Use utility/SaaS. Don't invest here.

⚠️ The #1 Mistake: Building Commodities

The most common strategic error is custom-building things that are already commodities. Building your own auth system, payment processor, or hosting infrastructure wastes engineering time on solved problems. The map makes this obvious — if it's on the right side, stop building it.

🔮 What Movement Means for You

➡️ When a component moves right: Switch from building to buying. What was your advantage becomes table stakes. Competitors will commoditize it.
⬆️ When a commodity enables new genesis: This is how innovation works. Cloud (commodity) enabled SaaS (product) which enabled AI apps (genesis). Each layer enables the next.

🔍 What the Map Reveals

AI audio feedback is the only genesis/custom component visible to users. That's where the competitive advantage lives. Everything else is product or commodity. The strategic implication: pour engineering resources into the AI, and outsource or adopt everything else. If Trala is custom-building gamification or payment flows, that's wasted effort.

🔮 Movement to Watch

AI audio feedback is evolving fast. Today it's custom-built (competitive advantage). In 2-3 years, off-the-shelf AI music APIs will exist (product stage). When that happens, Trala's advantage erodes unless they've moved to the next genesis component — maybe AI-powered personalized curriculum or emotional practice coaching.

When to Use

Right situations and wrong ones

✅ Use Wardley Mapping When...

  • ✔️ Build vs. buy decisions — should you build this or use a service?
  • ✔️ Tech stack planning — where to invest engineering effort
  • ✔️ Competitive strategy — understanding where advantage lives
  • ✔️ Anticipating disruption — what will commoditize next?
  • ✔️ Communicating strategy visually to leadership

⛔ Skip Wardley Mapping When...

  • ❌ You need feature-level prioritization (use RICE/ICE)
  • ❌ You need user insights (use JTBD or user research)
  • ❌ You're doing a PM interview (too complex for 30 min — mention it but don't draw one)
  • ❌ Your product is very simple with few components
  • ❌ You haven't done basic competitive analysis yet (use Porter's first)
