✏️ Design Comparison

Which Design Framework?

CIRCLES vs JTBD vs Design Thinking vs Story Mapping — when to use which, and how to combine them.

👁️

The 4 Design Frameworks at a Glance

Different tools for different stages of product thinking

🎯
CIRCLES
7-step method for answering "Design a product for X" interview questions. Structured thinking from problem to solution in 15-20 minutes.
Interview Feature ideation 15-20 min
💼
JTBD
Uncover why users "hire" your product. Shift from demographics to motivations. Reveals the real competition and unmet needs.
Strategy Research Ongoing
💎
Design Thinking
5-phase process: Empathize → Define → Ideate → Prototype → Test. Full discovery-to-validation cycle for complex, unclear problems.
Discovery Innovation 2-6 weeks
🗺️
Story Mapping
Visualize the full user journey, then slice it into releases. Turns a flat backlog into a 2D map that the whole team understands.
Planning Delivery 90 min session

🧠 The Key Difference

These aren't competing tools — they operate at different stages. JTBD asks "what job?", Design Thinking asks "what solution?", CIRCLES is DT compressed for interviews, and Story Mapping asks "what ships when?" Think of them as a pipeline, not a menu.

📐 The Pipeline View

In the real world, these chain together: JTBD (discover the job) → Design Thinking (empathize, ideate, prototype) → Story Mapping (plan what ships when) → CIRCLES (explain your thinking in interviews). Each one's output feeds the next one's input.

🏷️ One-Line Summary Each

CIRCLES: "Walk me through your thinking" — structured interview answer in 7 steps.
JTBD: "Don't sell drills, sell holes" — understand the job, not the user demographics.
Design Thinking: "Fall in love with the problem" — empathize, explore, test, iterate.
Story Mapping: "See the whole journey, then slice it" — from flat backlog to release plan.

🚦 Quick Decision Rules

If you're in an interview →
CIRCLES
If you don't know WHY users behave this way →
JTBD
If the problem is fuzzy and needs exploring →
Design Thinking
If you know WHAT to build but need to plan releases →
Story Mapping
In practice, you'll often use 2-3 together. The flowchart picks the starting point. Once you discover the job (JTBD), you might jump to Design Thinking to explore solutions, then Story Map to plan delivery.

Real-World Scenarios

8 situations → which framework wins

1

PM Interview: "Design a fitness app for seniors"

You have 35 minutes. The interviewer wants structured thinking, not a shipped product.
🎯 CIRCLES — walk through all 7 steps, show your PM thinking process
2

Trala users cancel after 2 weeks — why?

You have data showing churn but don't know the root cause. Need to understand user motivation.
💼 JTBD — run Switch Interviews to discover what job they hired Trala for and why they "fired" it
3

Leadership says "we need a social feature" but nobody knows what

Vague directive, unclear problem, no research yet. Stakeholders have different ideas of what "social" means.
💎 Design Thinking — Empathize with users first, Define the real need, then Ideate and Prototype options
4

You have 40 features in the backlog for the new practice mode

Engineering asks "what do we build first?" You know the features but need to organize them into coherent releases.
🗺️ Story Mapping — lay out the user journey, place features under each step, draw release lines
5

Skoove wants to enter the kids' market

New audience, different needs, no existing data. Need to understand if this is viable and what it looks like.
💼 JTBD first (what job would parents/kids hire a piano app for?) → then 💎 Design Thinking (explore solutions)
6

Interview: "How would you improve Spotify's discover weekly?"

Product improvement question, 30-minute interview format. Interviewer expects structure.
🎯 CIRCLES — Comprehend the feature, Identify users, define needs, then List + Evaluate solutions with metrics
7

Onboarding redesign — new team members don't understand the full product

The product has grown organically. No single view of the user experience exists.
🗺️ Story Mapping — create the map together as a team exercise; the act of mapping IS the onboarding
8

Users request "offline mode" — should you build it?

The feature request is specific but you're unsure about the real need behind it.
💼 JTBD — investigate the job: is it "practice on the subway" or "avoid data usage"? The solution might not be offline mode at all

🔑 Pattern to Notice

Unclear WHY → JTBD. Unclear WHAT → Design Thinking. Unclear WHEN → Story Mapping. Interview → CIRCLES. Most real problems combine 2-3.

🔄 3 Power Combos

JTBD + Story Mapping
Best for: Feature-rich product that's losing focus
JTBD reveals the core job → Story Map organizes features around that job → Release slices ensure every version delivers on the job. Example: Skoove has 200 features. JTBD reveals the job is "feel progress fast." Story Map reprioritizes everything around that job.
Design Thinking + Story Mapping
Best for: New product or major redesign
Design Thinking generates and validates solutions → Story Map turns the validated ideas into a release plan. Example: Trala onboarding redesign — DT discovers "play a song in 5 min" wins → Story Map plans MVP, v2, v3 of the new onboarding.
JTBD + Design Thinking + Story Mapping
Best for: Entering a new market
All three in sequence. JTBD interviews reveal the job in the new market → DT explores what the product looks like for that job → Story Map plans the first release. Example: Skoove entering kids' market — JTBD with parents, DT workshops with kids, Story Map for kids' MVP.
