CIRCLES Method

A 7-step framework for answering any product design question. The PM interview gold standard.

⚡

In 15 seconds: CIRCLES gives you a repeatable 7-step structure to answer "Design a product for X" questions. Comprehend the situation, Identify users, Report their needs, Cut to one priority, List solutions, Evaluate tradeoffs, Summarize your recommendation. Works in interviews and real product work.

🧩

The 7 Steps

Follow this order every time — build the habit

C

Comprehend the Situation

"What exactly are we solving?"

Clarify the prompt. Ask questions. Understand constraints, goals, and the company context before touching any solutions.

I

Identify the Users

"Who are we building for?"

List user segments. Pick one primary persona to focus on. You can't design for everyone — choose.

R

Report Their Needs

"What are their pain points?"

List the top 3–5 needs or pain points of your chosen user. Prioritize by severity and frequency.

C

Cut Through Prioritization

"Which need do we solve first?"

Pick ONE pain point to focus on. Justify why this one wins. Use impact, reach, or urgency as criteria.

L

List Solutions

"How could we solve this?"

Brainstorm 3–5 solution ideas. Range from simple to ambitious. Don't judge yet — just generate.

E

Evaluate Tradeoffs

"Which solution wins and why?"

Compare solutions on impact, effort, risk, and alignment. Pick one and explain the tradeoffs clearly.

S

Summarize

"Here's what I recommend."

Recap: user → problem → solution → why. Mention metrics to track and next steps. Crisp 30-second pitch.

🧠 Memory trick

The flow mirrors how a detective works: understand the scene → identify the person → discover the problem → narrow the lead → explore options → weigh evidence → present the case.

🔍

Each Step in Detail

What to do, what to say, how long to spend

⏱ 2–3 min

C
Comprehend

"Before I jump in, let me make sure I understand the problem..."

Ask clarifying questions to nail down: What's the goal? Who's the company? What are the constraints (time, budget, platform)? What does success look like?

Questions to ask: "What metric are we optimizing for?" · "Is this mobile, web, or both?" · "Who's the target audience?" · "What's the timeline?" · "Are there competitors doing this?"
⏱ 2–3 min

I
Identify Users

"Let me think about who uses this..."

List 2–4 user segments. For each, note their context, goals, and tech comfort. Then explicitly pick ONE to focus on and explain why.

Template: "I see three types of users: [A], [B], and [C]. I'll focus on [A] because they have the highest pain + largest segment."
⏱ 2–3 min

R
Report Needs

"For this user, the biggest pain points are..."

List 3–5 needs or pain points. Use: "They need to [verb] because [reason]" format. Ground them in real behavior, not assumptions.

Pro tip: Frame needs as problems, not solutions. "They need to find friends easily" not "They need a search bar."
⏱ 1–2 min

C
Cut / Prioritize

"If I had to pick one, I'd focus on..."

Pick the #1 pain point. Justify with a clear reason: highest impact, affects most users, aligns with company goals, or biggest gap vs. competitors.

Template: "I'll focus on [need] because it affects the most users and directly impacts [metric]."
⏱ 3–5 min

L
List Solutions

"Here are a few ways we could solve this..."

Generate 3 solutions: one simple/fast, one medium, one ambitious. Describe each briefly — what the user sees and does. This is your creative moment.

Range them: "Quick win: [simple]. Better: [medium]. Best: [ambitious]." This shows product thinking, not just design thinking.
⏱ 2–3 min

E
Evaluate Tradeoffs

"Comparing these options..."

Compare on: user impact, implementation effort, risk, alignment with goals. Pick a winner and explain what you're sacrificing. Tradeoff awareness is the signal interviewers want.

Template: "I'd go with [B] because it has [X impact] for [Y effort]. [A] is faster but doesn't solve the core problem. [C] is ideal long-term but too risky for now."
⏱ 1–2 min

S
Summarize

"To wrap up..."

Recap in 30 seconds: who the user is, what problem you're solving, your recommended solution, why it wins, and how you'd measure success.

Template: "We're solving [problem] for [user] by building [solution]. I'd measure success with [metric] and test it by [MVP approach]."

⏱ Total time: ~15–20 minutes

In a 35-minute PM interview, CIRCLES takes about 18 minutes. This leaves ~5 min for questions at the start and ~10 min for follow-up discussion. Don't rush — but don't linger on any single step either.

Full Walkthrough

"Design a feature for elderly users to use ride-sharing"

🎤 "Design a feature for elderly users to use ride-sharing apps more easily."

C
Comprehend

Clarify: Is this for an existing app like Uber/Lyft or a new product? What defines "elderly"? Are we focused on booking, navigation, or the full journey?
I'll assume we're designing for Uber, targeting users 70+, and focusing on the booking experience since that's the highest friction point for this demographic.

I
Identify Users

Three segments: (A) Tech-comfortable seniors who use smartphones daily, (B) Reluctant tech users who only use basic apps, (C) Non-tech users who depend on family for help.
I'll focus on Group B — reluctant tech users. They HAVE a smartphone but find complex UIs overwhelming. This is the largest group with the most room for improvement.

R
Report Needs

Top pain points: (1) Too many steps to book a ride. (2) Small text and confusing map interface. (3) Anxiety about getting into a stranger's car. (4) Difficulty communicating with drivers. (5) No easy way for family to monitor trips.
These users need a simpler flow, larger UI elements, safety reassurance, and a connection to their support network.

C
Cut

Pain #1 (too many steps) is the gateway problem — if they can't even book the ride, nothing else matters.
I'll focus on reducing booking friction because it's the top-of-funnel blocker. If we fix this, more elderly users complete their first ride, which unlocks retention.

L
List Solutions

A) One-tap favorites: Save frequent destinations (doctor, grocery, home). One giant button per destination. B) Voice booking: "Take me to Dr. Smith" via voice command. C) Family booking: A loved one pre-schedules rides remotely from their own app.
Three solutions ranging from simple UI change (A) to voice AI (B) to multi-user coordination (C).

E
Evaluate

A is easiest to build and test — low effort, clear impact. B is exciting but voice UX is hard and error-prone for elderly users. C is powerful but requires a second user to adopt the feature.
I'd recommend A (one-tap favorites) as the MVP. It solves the core friction with minimal complexity. We can layer B and C later as the user base grows.

S
Summarize

We're solving ride-booking friction for reluctant-tech elderly users (70+) by adding one-tap favorite destinations — giant buttons for "Home," "Doctor," "Grocery" that book a ride in one tap. I'd measure success with first-ride completion rate for 70+ users and weekly active bookings. MVP: add 3 preset buttons to the home screen, test with 500 users in one city over 4 weeks.

🔍 Why this answer works

Notice the funnel: started broad (all elderly), narrowed to one segment, narrowed to one problem, explored multiple solutions, picked the simplest effective one, and ended with measurable success criteria. That's CIRCLES in action.

Practice It

Work through a prompt step by step

🎤 "Design a feature for a fitness app that helps users stay motivated"
Aim for 3–5 specific questions. Avoid generic ones.
Name the segment and explain WHY you chose it.
Frame as problems, not solutions. "They struggle to..." not "They need a button."
Justify with impact, reach, or alignment.
Range from simple to creative. Don't self-censor yet.
Name what you're sacrificing. Tradeoff awareness = PM signal.
Practice saying this out loud. Under 30 seconds.
The best practice is timed. Set a 20-minute timer and work through the whole thing without stopping. Repeat with 3–5 different prompts.

When to Use

Built for interviews, useful beyond them

✅ Use CIRCLES when...

  • PM interview — "Design a product/feature for X"
  • New feature ideation when you need structure
  • Stakeholder says "We should build X" — CIRCLES helps you think before committing
  • Exploring a problem space you're unfamiliar with
  • Teaching junior PMs how to think about product design

🚫 Skip CIRCLES when...

  • You already understand the problem deeply — just design
  • Urgent bug fix or fire — not the time for a structured exercise
  • Prioritizing between known features → use RICE/ICE instead
  • Technical design (architecture, APIs) → different toolset
  • The problem is well-defined and you just need solutions

🔗 How CIRCLES connects to other frameworks

CIRCLES generates the feature ideas. Then RICE/ICE ranks them. And Kano tells you what type they are. CIRCLES is the beginning of the product pipeline — discovery, not delivery.

⚠️

6 Common Pitfalls

Most people fail at steps 1–2, not 5–7

1

Skipping Comprehend

Jumping to solutions immediately. The interviewer gave you a vague prompt ON PURPOSE — they want to see you clarify.

Force yourself to ask 3–5 questions before touching anything else. "Let me make sure I understand..." buys you clarity AND time.
2

Designing for "everyone"

Not picking a specific user. "All users" means you design for no one.

Name 2–3 segments, explicitly pick ONE, and say why. "I'll focus on segment B because..."
3

Listing solutions as needs

"They need a search bar" is a solution, not a need. "They can't find what they're looking for" is the need.

Use "They struggle to..." or "They need to [verb]" format. Never name UI elements in the Needs step.
4

Only one solution

Going straight to your best idea. This looks like you can't think broadly. Interviewers want to see creative range.

Always generate 3: quick win, medium, ambitious. Even if you know which one wins, show the range first.
5

No tradeoffs in Evaluate

"Solution B is the best" with no comparison. Tradeoff awareness is THE signal interviewers screen for.

For each option, state what you gain AND what you lose. "B has higher impact but takes longer. A is faster but doesn't fully solve the problem."
6

No metrics in Summary

Great recommendation but no way to measure success. How would you know if it worked?

End with: "I'd measure success with [metric] and test with [MVP approach]." This shows product rigor.

Using CIRCLES in an Interview

35-minute product design question — here's the timeline

0–3

C — Comprehend

Listen to the prompt. Ask 3–5 clarifying questions. Restate your understanding: "So I'm designing [X] for [Y] to achieve [Z]."

3–6

I — Identify Users

Name 2–3 user segments. Pick one. Say: "I'll focus on [segment] because [reason]." Pause for interviewer nod.

6–9

R — Report Needs

List 3–5 pain points for your chosen user. Use real scenarios: "When they try to [action], they struggle because..."

9–11

C — Cut

Pick one pain point. "I'll focus on [need] because it has the highest impact on [metric]." Brief justification.

11–18

L — List Solutions

This is your creative showcase. Spend the most time here. Describe 3 solutions with enough detail that the interviewer can visualize them.

18–22

E — Evaluate

Compare solutions on impact vs effort. State tradeoffs clearly. "I recommend [B] because [reasoning]. The tradeoff is [sacrifice]."

22–24

S — Summarize

30-second recap: user → problem → solution → metric → MVP. Crisp and confident.

24–35

Follow-up Discussion

Interviewer digs into your choices. Be ready to defend or pivot. "That's a great point — if we consider [X], we might shift to..."

💡 Interview tips

Signal each step verbally: "Now let me identify the users..." This shows structure and lets the interviewer follow your thinking.

Check in after step 2: "Does this user focus make sense, or would you like me to explore a different segment?" This shows collaboration.

Don't memorize — internalize. Interviewers can spot rehearsed answers. Practice the framework enough that it's natural, not robotic.

Quick Quiz

6 questions to lock it in

1. What's the FIRST thing you should do when you hear a product design prompt?

Always Comprehend first. The prompt is vague on purpose. Clarifying shows you think before you build.

2. "They need a search bar" — is this a need or a solution?

A search bar is a solution. The need is: "Users struggle to find specific items." Always frame needs as problems, not UI elements.

3. How many solutions should you list in the L step?

Always 3 — ranging from simple to ambitious. This shows creative range and product thinking. One idea looks narrow; too many wastes time.

4. What's the most important signal in the Evaluate step?

Tradeoff awareness is THE PM signal. "B has higher impact but costs more. A is faster but doesn't fully solve it." This shows you think in tradeoffs, not absolutes.

5. Your Summary should end with...

End with: "I'd measure success with [metric] and test with [MVP]." This shows you think in terms of outcomes, not just outputs.

6. After CIRCLES generates feature ideas, what framework helps you rank them?

CIRCLES generates ideas. RICE/ICE ranks them. Kano categorizes them. They're complementary — CIRCLES is discovery, the others are prioritization.
