Jobs-to-be-Done

People don't buy products. They hire them to get a job done.

The Job Story Format

→
I want to...

So I can...

⚡

In 15 seconds: JTBD reframes product thinking. Instead of "what features should we add?" ask "what job is the user hiring this product to do?" A milkshake competes with a banana, a bagel, and boredom — not just other milkshakes. Understanding the job reveals your real competitors and real opportunities.

💡

The Core Idea

It's not about the product. It's about the progress.

❌ Product Thinking

"What features does our product have?"
"What do users do in our app?"
"How can we improve our product?"

→

✅ JTBD Thinking

"What progress is the user trying to make?"
"What did they fire to hire us?"
"Why would they switch away?"

🥤 The Milkshake Story

Clayton Christensen studied why people buy milkshakes in the morning. Demographics were useless — the buyers had nothing in common. But the job was identical: they needed something to make a boring commute less boring, that lasted 20 minutes, that they could consume with one hand, and that kept them full until lunch.

The competitors weren't other milkshakes. They were bananas, bagels, boredom, and Snickers bars. Once you see the job, you see the real competition — and it's never who you think.

🧠 Three Dimensions of Every Job

⚙️ FunctionalThe practical task. "Get from A to B." "Learn a song."
💜 EmotionalHow they want to feel. "Feel confident." "Reduce anxiety."
👥 SocialHow they want to be seen. "Look like a good parent." "Seem professional."
Most PMs only think about functional jobs. Emotional and social jobs often drive the purchase decision more than the functional one.

🔑 The key mindset shift

Users don't want your product. They want the progress it enables. Your product is just the current best candidate for the job. If something better shows up, they'll fire you and hire that instead.

The Job Statement

A precise formula for writing jobs

The Job Story Formula

🎯
When I...
Situation / Trigger
The specific moment that creates the need. Context matters.
→
⚡
I want to...
Motivation / Action
What progress they're trying to make. Solution-free.
→
✨
So I can...
Desired Outcome
The end state — functional, emotional, or social.

✍️ Examples

When I have a long boring commute, I want to consume something satisfying with one hand, so I can make the drive less tedious and stay full until lunch.
When I want to learn an instrument as an adult, I want to play recognizable songs quickly, so I can feel a sense of accomplishment and impress my family.
When I arrive in a new city for work, I want to get to my hotel without confusion, so I can feel in control and arrive relaxed before my meeting.

📐 Rules for good job statements

1. Solution-free. No product names, no features, no UI elements.
2. Stable over time. The job exists before and after your product.
3. Specific situation. "When I..." grounds the job in a real moment.
4. Includes emotional/social outcome. "So I can feel confident" is as valid as "so I can save 10 minutes."

⚠️ The #1 mistake

Writing "When I open the app, I want to see a dashboard, so I can track my progress." That's a feature description disguised as a job. The real job: "When I'm trying to build a new habit, I want to see evidence that I'm improving, so I can stay motivated."

Real Examples

Product thinking vs JTBD thinking across industries

🎵 Music Learning

Skoove / Trala

Users want more lessons and better content
When I have 15 free minutes, I want to feel like I made progress on an instrument, so I can feel accomplished and creative
Competition: YouTube, books, Netflix (for the free time)
🚗 Ride-sharing

Uber

Users want cheaper rides and more drivers
When I need to get somewhere and don't want to deal with parking, I want reliable transport, so I can arrive stress-free and on time
Competition: own car, subway, walking, canceling the trip
💼 Job Search

LinkedIn

Users want more connections and a better profile
When I'm considering a career change, I want to understand what's out there, so I can make a confident move at the right time
Competition: recruiters, word-of-mouth, staying put
📦 E-commerce

Amazon

Users want more products and faster shipping
When I realize I need something, I want to solve it immediately without leaving the house, so I can get back to what I was doing
Competition: local store, borrowing, doing without
📱 Social

Instagram

Users want filters and more followers
When something interesting happens, I want to share a curated version of it, so I can feel seen and connected
Competition: texting, calling, doing nothing
☕ Coffee

Starbucks

Users want better coffee and more variety
When I need a mental break between tasks, I want a ritual that feels like a reward, so I can reset and face the next thing
Competition: water, a walk, scrolling phone, napping

🔍 Notice the pattern

In every example, the real competition isn't what you'd expect. Starbucks doesn't just compete with other coffee shops — it competes with anything that gives you a mental reset.

When to Use JTBD

Strategy tool, not sprint tool

✅ Use JTBD when...

  • Defining product strategy — "What business are we really in?"
  • Users are churning and you don't know why
  • Entering a new market — who are the real competitors?
  • Building messaging/positioning — what progress do users care about?
  • Evaluating an acquisition or partnership
  • PM interview asks "How would you improve X?" — frame with the job first

🚫 Skip JTBD when...

  • You need to prioritize a known backlog → use RICE/ICE
  • You need specific UI decisions → use user testing
  • Time pressure — sprint planning isn't the time for JTBD research
  • The job is obvious and well-understood by the whole team
  • You're optimizing, not discovering (A/B test instead)

🔗 How JTBD connects to other frameworks

JTBD → CIRCLESJTBD tells you the job. CIRCLES helps you design the solution. Start with "what job?" then use CIRCLES to answer "how to solve it?"
JTBD → KanoJTBD reveals the job. Kano shows which features are Must-Haves vs Delighters for that job. Kano operates within a JTBD context.
JTBD → RICEJTBD identifies the most important jobs. RICE scores which features best serve those jobs. JTBD scopes what RICE ranks.
JTBD → Value vs EffortJTBD redefines "Value" — value isn't feature quality, it's how well you serve the job. This makes the Value axis meaningful.

6 Common Pitfalls

How most people get JTBD wrong

1

Writing solutions, not jobs

"When I open the app, I want to see my dashboard." That's a feature request wearing a JTBD costume. The job exists independent of your product.

Remove all product/feature language. Test: would this job statement make sense 50 years ago? If yes, it's a real job.
2

Too abstract

"I want to live a better life." True, but useless for product decisions. Job statements need a specific situation trigger.

Always include "When I..." — the situation makes it actionable. "When I get home exhausted" is 10x more useful than "I want to relax."
3

Ignoring emotional and social jobs

Only focusing on the functional task. "Get from A to B" misses why someone chooses a BMW over a Toyota.

For every functional job, ask: "How does the user want to FEEL?" and "How does the user want to be SEEN?" Those often drive the decision.
4

Confusing the job with the job executor

Defining jobs by demographics: "Millennials want..." or "Enterprise users need..." The same person hires different products for different jobs in different contexts.

Jobs are defined by situations, not personas. A CEO and a student can have the same job: "When I'm stuck on a problem, I want a quick second opinion."
5

Not identifying competing "hires"

Only looking at direct competitors. If you're a music learning app, your competitor isn't just other music apps — it's Netflix, YouTube, going for a walk.

Ask: "What was the user doing BEFORE they hired our product? What else could they hire instead?" Map the full competitive landscape.
6

Using JTBD as a one-time exercise

Running one JTBD session, creating a poster, and forgetting about it. Jobs evolve. Context changes. New competitors emerge.

Revisit JTBD quarterly. Use it as a lens in every product review: "Are we still solving the right job? Has the job changed?"

Running JTBD Interviews

The Switch Interview — how to uncover the real job

🔄 The Switch Interview

The most powerful JTBD technique. You interview people who recently switched — either TO your product or AWAY from it. You reconstruct the full decision timeline: what pushed them away from the old solution, what pulled them toward the new one, and what anxieties almost stopped them.

⚡ The Four Forces of Progress

Every switch decision is driven by 4 forces — 2 pushing toward change, 2 resisting it:

→ Forces FOR change
Push: Frustration with current solution. "I'm so tired of how slow this is."
Pull: Attraction of new solution. "That looks so much easier."
← Forces AGAINST change
Anxiety: Fear of the unknown. "What if it's worse? What if I lose my data?"
Habit: Comfort of the current. "I know how this works. It's fine."
To win, Push + Pull must overcome Anxiety + Habit. Your product can increase Pull and reduce Anxiety — but you can't force Push or eliminate Habit. Understanding all four is critical.
Phase 1
5 min

Set the scene

"I'd love to understand your experience choosing [product]. There are no right or wrong answers — I'm interested in the full story."

Phase 2
10 min

First thought — the push

"Tell me about the first time you thought about switching. What was happening? What were you frustrated with?" Dig into the specific moment. Where were they? What triggered it?

Phase 3
10 min

Looking around — the pull

"What did you look at? Who did you talk to? What was appealing about the alternatives?" Map every option they considered, including doing nothing.

Phase 4
10 min

Anxiety & habit

"What almost stopped you? What worried you about switching? Was there anything you'd miss from the old way?" This reveals the barriers you need to reduce.

Phase 5
10 min

The decision & aftermath

"What was the final tipping point? Has it met your expectations? Would you go back?" Capture the moment of commitment and whether the job is being done well.

🎯 Key Interview Questions

🔍 To find the Push: "What was the hardest part about how you were doing it before?"
"When did you first feel frustrated enough to look for something else?"
"Can you walk me through that specific moment?"
🧲 To find the Pull: "What caught your attention about the alternative?"
"What did you imagine your life would be like after switching?"
"What was the 'aha' that made you say 'this is the one'?"
😰 To find Anxiety: "What almost stopped you from switching?"
"Were you worried about losing anything?"
"Did anyone try to talk you out of it?"
🛋️ To find Habit: "What did you like about the old way?"
"Was there anything comforting about how you used to do it?"
"Did you ever think 'this is good enough, why bother'?"

📊 How many interviews?

8–12 switch interviews is usually enough to see patterns. You'll hear the same forces repeat by interview 7–8. Focus on recent switchers (last 3 months) — memory fades fast and you want specific moments, not rationalized summaries.

Quick Quiz

7 questions to lock it in

1. "People don't want a quarter-inch drill. They want a..."

The product (drill) is hired to do a job (make a hole). If someone invented a laser pointer that made perfect holes, the drill is fired.

2. Which is a proper JTBD job statement?

Good job statements are solution-free, situation-specific, and include a desired outcome. No product names, no demographics.

3. What are the three dimensions of a job?

Every job has a functional task (get from A to B), an emotional need (feel safe), and a social dimension (look like I have my life together).

4. In the milkshake story, what was the real competition?

The job was "make a boring commute less boring and stay full." Anything that serves that job is competition — regardless of product category.

5. What are the 4 forces in a Switch Interview?

Push and Pull drive change. Anxiety and Habit resist it. For someone to switch, Push + Pull must overcome Anxiety + Habit.

6. How many switch interviews do you typically need to see patterns?

8–12 is the sweet spot. Patterns usually emerge by interview 7–8. Focus on people who switched recently (last 3 months).

7. JTBD generates insights about the JOB. What framework then RANKS the features that serve it?

JTBD discovers the job. CIRCLES designs solutions for it. RICE/ICE ranks those solutions. Each framework has its lane.
