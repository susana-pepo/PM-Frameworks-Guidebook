In 15 seconds: Five forces determine how profitable and competitive a market is. Analyze all 5 to understand why your market works the way it does and where you have power (or don't). Created by Michael Porter (Harvard, 1979).

🏭

The 5 Forces

What shapes competition in any market

THE 5 COMPETITIVE FORCES
⚔️
1. Competitive Rivalry
How intense is competition among existing players? More rivals + similar products = lower profits for everyone.
🚪
2. Threat of New Entrants
How easy is it for new competitors to enter? Low barriers = constant new threats eating your share.
🔄
3. Threat of Substitutes
Can users solve the same problem a completely different way? Substitutes cap what you can charge.
🛒
4. Buyer Power
How much leverage do customers have? Many alternatives + zero switching cost = buyers dictate terms.
🏭
5. Supplier Power
How much leverage do suppliers/partners have? Few suppliers or unique inputs = they control your costs.

💬 Michael Porter's Key Idea

Strategy is NOT about being the best. It's about being different in a way that lets you earn sustainable profits. The 5 Forces show you where the profit goes in your industry — and whether you can keep any of it.

🧠 Memory Trick: The Battlefield

Think of your company in the center of a battlefield. Threats come from 4 directions — new entrants from the north, substitutes from the south, buyers from the east, suppliers from the west — and you're already fighting rivals on the field itself.

Rate each force as Low / Medium / High. High forces = profit pressure. The goal isn't to "win" all 5 — it's to understand which ones shape your strategic choices.
🔍

Analyzing Each Force

What to look for and how to rate each one

⚔️ Force 1: Competitive Rivalry

Key question: How intense is competition among existing players?

HIGH when:
• Many competitors of similar size
• Slow industry growth
• Low differentiation (commodity)
• High exit barriers
LOW when:
• Clear market leader
• Fast-growing market
• Strong differentiation
• High switching costs

🚪 Force 2: Threat of New Entrants

Key question: How easy is it for newcomers to enter?

HIGH threat when:
• Low capital requirements
• Easy to copy (no patents/IP)
• No network effects
• Weak brand loyalty
LOW threat when:
• High upfront investment
• Strong IP/patents
• Network effects / data moats
• Regulatory barriers

🔄 Force 3: Threat of Substitutes

Key question: Can users solve their problem a completely different way?

HIGH threat when:
• Many alternatives exist
• Low switching costs
• Substitute is cheaper/easier
• Users are price-sensitive
LOW threat when:
• No close alternatives
• High switching costs
• Product deeply integrated
• Emotional attachment/habit

🛒 Force 4: Buyer Power

Key question: How much leverage do your customers have?

HIGH buyer power when:
• Few, large buyers
• Product is undifferentiated
• Low switching costs
• Buyers are well-informed
LOW buyer power when:
• Many small buyers
• Unique/differentiated product
• High switching costs
• Strong brand loyalty

🏭 Force 5: Supplier Power

Key question: How much leverage do your suppliers/partners have?

HIGH supplier power when:
• Few suppliers
• Unique/specialized inputs
• High switching costs
• Supplier can forward-integrate
LOW supplier power when:
• Many suppliers available
• Commodity inputs
• Easy to switch
• You're a large customer

Example: Music Learning Apps

Porter's 5 Forces for Skoove/Trala's market

Online music learning apps (Skoove, Trala, Simply Piano, Yousician, Flowkey) compete in the digital music education market. Let's analyze the 5 forces.

⚔

Competitive Rivalry: HIGH

Many apps with similar features (interactive lessons, sheet music, feedback). Yousician, Simply Piano, Flowkey, Skoove, Trala all target the same beginner segment. Low differentiation makes this a crowded fight.
🚪

New Entrants: MEDIUM-HIGH

Building a music learning app is increasingly accessible (AI tools, content libraries). Low capital barrier. BUT: content library depth and music licensing create some moat. Network effects are weak.
🔄

Substitutes: HIGH

Substitutes are everywhere: YouTube tutorials (free), private teachers ($40-80/hr), books, masterclasses, TikTok. Users can "hire" many alternatives for the same job. The most dangerous substitute: free YouTube.
🛒

Buyer Power: HIGH

Users have many choices and zero switching costs (cancel monthly, download competitor). Price sensitivity is high in a consumer market. One-click churn. App Store reviews create full transparency.
🏭

Supplier Power: MEDIUM

Music publishers and rights holders have power over licensed content. Platform stores (Apple/Google) take 30%. BUT: growing royalty-free content library, and original content can be created in-house.

🎯 Strategic Implication

This is a tough market (4 of 5 forces are medium-high+). The strategic response: differentiate hard (instrument-specific depth like Trala = violin only), reduce substitution threat (offer what YouTube can't — real-time feedback), and increase switching costs (saved progress, personalized paths).

When to Use / Skip

Right tool, right moment

✅ Use Porter's When...

  • Entering a new market — understand competitive dynamics before committing resources
  • Strategic planning — annual or quarterly strategy reviews
  • Pricing decisions — understand what the market will bear and why
  • Investor pitch — show you understand the competitive landscape
  • Partnership evaluation — assess supplier/buyer power dynamics

🚫 Skip It When...

  • Feature-level decisions — too high-level for "should we add dark mode?"
  • Very early-stage markets — forces shift weekly, analysis expires fast
  • Internal product issues — won't help with tech debt or team dynamics
  • You need user insights — Porter looks at market structure, not user needs (use JTBD)

🔗 Connections to Other Frameworks

Porter → Blue Ocean: Porter diagnoses the Red Ocean. Blue Ocean shows you how to escape it.
Porter → Playing to Win: "Where to Play" is informed by which markets have attractive force dynamics.
Porter → JTBD: Porter identifies substitutes at market level. JTBD reveals them at user motivation level.
Porter → Wardley: Both analyze competitive landscape, but Wardley adds evolution (how forces change over time).

Common Pitfalls

5 mistakes that weaken your analysis

1

Confusing Competitors with Substitutes

Listing Spotify as a "competitor" to a music learning app. Spotify is a substitute (different product, same job). Competitors offer the same type of product (Yousician vs Skoove).
Competitors = same product category. Substitutes = different product, same user job.
2

Static Analysis

Analyzing forces once and treating them as permanent. Markets shift. AI is lowering entry barriers in music ed RIGHT NOW. Your 2024 analysis may be wrong by 2026.
Re-run the analysis every 6-12 months, or when a major market event happens.
3

Ignoring Supplier Power

PMs often skip this force because "we build software, we don't have suppliers." But you do: app stores (30% cut), cloud providers, content licensors, payment processors.
List every external dependency. Each one has leverage over you.
4

Market Definition Too Broad

Analyzing "the education market" instead of "online instrument learning apps for adults." Too broad = meaningless averages. Too narrow = missing threats.
Define your market at the level where competitors actually compete for the same customers.
5

Using It for Feature Decisions

Trying to use Porter's to decide whether to build a metronome feature. This framework is about market structure, not product features.
Use Porter's for WHERE to compete. Use RICE/Kano for WHAT to build.
