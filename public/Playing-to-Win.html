The 5 Strategic Choices

Strategy is a set of choices, not a plan

The Core Idea

Most "strategies" are really just goals ("grow 20%") or activities ("launch mobile app"). A.G. Lafley and Roger Martin argue that real strategy is a set of integrated choices about where and how to compete — choices that reinforce each other.

The key word is choice. Strategy means choosing what you won't do as much as what you will. If you're trying to serve everyone, you have no strategy.

🏆
1. Winning Aspiration

What does "winning" mean for us? Not revenue targets — a purpose. "Help every adult learn an instrument" is an aspiration. "Grow 15%" is not.

📍
2. Where to Play

Which specific playing field? Geography, customer segment, product category, channel, vertical stage. The more specific, the better.

⚔️
3. How to Win

What's your competitive advantage in your chosen space? Only two real options: cost leadership (cheapest) or differentiation (unique value).

💪
4. Capabilities

What must you be world-class at to win this way? These are the 3-5 things you must invest in heavily. Everything else is secondary.

⚙️
5. Management Systems

What processes, metrics, and structures make sure choices stick? OKRs, hiring profiles, team structure, review cadences — the "operating system."

🧠 Memory Trick

Think of it as a funnel: the aspiration is the wide opening (purpose), it narrows to where you'll play, then how you'll win there, then what you need to be great at, then the systems that lock it in. Each choice constrains the next. They cascade.

💧

The Strategic Cascade

Why these choices must be integrated

Integration is the Whole Point

The most common mistake: treating each choice independently. "Our aspiration is X, we play everywhere, we win by being better at everything." That's not strategy — it's a wishlist.

Real strategy constrains. If your aspiration is to help adults learn piano, you shouldn't play in the kids market. If you win through depth of learning, you shouldn't invest in broad instrument coverage.

🔍 How Choices Constrain Each Other

🏆 Aspiration → 📍 Where
"Help every adult learn an instrument" → rules out kids market, academic settings, and professional musicians.
📍 Where → ⚔️ How
"Adult beginners via mobile apps" → can't win on price (too many free options). Must differentiate on learning quality.
⚔️ How → 💪 Capabilities
"Win through learning quality" → must be world-class at audio recognition, adaptive pedagogy, and habit design.
💪 Capabilities → ⚙️ Systems
"World-class audio recognition" → need ML engineers, real-time processing infra, and learning outcome metrics (not just DAU).

🚩 Signs Your Cascade Is Broken

❌ "We play everywhere" — If you haven't said no to any market, you haven't made a Where choice.
❌ "We win by being the best" — Best at what, specifically? "Best" isn't a strategy.
❌ "We need to be great at everything" — If you have 10 capabilities, you have zero priorities.
❌ "Our strategy is to grow 20%" — That's a goal, not a strategy. How will you grow?

💡 The Lafley Test

Read your strategy out loud. If a competitor could copy-paste it and it would still make sense for them, you don't have a strategy. Real strategy is specific enough that it only works for you.

Deep Dive: Where & How

The two choices that matter most

Lafley and Martin call Where to Play and How to Win the "heart of strategy." The other three choices support these two. Let's break them down.

📍 Where to Play — 5 Dimensions

🌍 Geography
Which markets, countries, regions?
👥 Customer Segment
Which users? Demographics, behaviors, needs?
📦 Product Category
Which products or services? Which NOT?
🛒 Channel
How do you reach them? Direct, retail, platform?
🔗 Value Chain Stage
Which part do you own? Create, distribute, service?

⚔️ How to Win — Only 2 Real Options

💰
Cost Leadership

Deliver acceptable value at the lowest cost. Win through efficiency, scale, and simplicity. Examples: IKEA, Ryanair, Walmart.

Danger: race to bottom
✨
Differentiation

Deliver unique value that justifies premium pricing. Win through innovation, brand, or experience. Examples: Apple, Dyson, Peloton.

Danger: niche too small

⚠️ The "Stuck in the Middle" Trap

Trying to be both cheap AND differentiated usually means you're neither. You end up with mediocre quality at mediocre prices. Pick one. (Blue Ocean's "value innovation" is the rare exception where you break this trade-off.)

Real Example

Playing to Win for Trala / Skoove

🎻 Trala's Strategic Cascade

🏆 Winning Aspiration

"Make learning violin accessible to every adult who's ever wanted to try, by providing a teacher-quality experience in an app."

📍 Where to Play

Segment: Adult beginners (25-55) in English-speaking markets. Channel: Mobile (iOS first). Category: Violin only. NOT: kids, professionals, schools, multi-instrument.

⚔️ How to Win

Differentiation: Real-time audio feedback that's as good as a human teacher. Not competing on library size (YouTube wins), not on price (free apps exist). Win through learning quality and outcomes.

💪 Capabilities

1. Audio recognition ML (pitch, rhythm, intonation). 2. Pedagogical content design (not just songs — structured learning). 3. Habit formation UX (streaks, progress, motivation). 4. Instrument-specific expertise (violin is different from piano).

⚙️ Management Systems

Metrics: Lessons completed (not just DAU), 30-day retention, learning milestones hit. Process: Content reviewed by real violin teachers. Hiring: Prioritize ML engineers + music educators over growth hackers.

🔍 Notice the Integration

Every choice reinforces the others. "Violin only" (Where) enables "best audio recognition" (Capability). "Learning quality" (How to Win) drives "lessons completed" as the metric (Systems). If you changed one choice, the others would need to change too. That's the cascade working.

When to Use

Right situations and wrong ones

✅ Use Playing to Win When...

  • ✔️ Defining or refining product strategy
  • ✔️ Team can't agree on priorities (cascade forces alignment)
  • ✔️ Annual/quarterly planning sessions
  • ✔️ PM interview asks "how would you approach strategy for X?"
  • ✔️ New product launch — need to define the playing field

⛔ Skip Playing to Win When...

  • ❌ You need feature-level prioritization (use RICE/ICE)
  • ❌ You need user insights (use JTBD or user research)
  • ❌ You need competitive diagnosis (use Porter's first)
  • ❌ You need design solutions (use Design Thinking)
  • ❌ You're in early discovery — strategy needs data first

🔗 Connections to Other Frameworks

Porter's
Input: Porter diagnoses the competitive landscape. Playing to Win uses that to choose Where to Play and How to Win.
Blue Ocean
Inspiration → Execution: Blue Ocean shows the opportunity. Playing to Win turns it into specific, actionable choices.
OKRs
Strategy → Execution: OKRs operationalize the Management Systems choice into quarterly objectives.
Wardley
Static → Dynamic: Playing to Win defines choices now. Wardley Mapping shows how they'll need to evolve.

📋 Typical Strategic Flow

Porter's (diagnose) → Blue Ocean (find space) → Playing to Win (make choices) → Wardley (plan evolution) → OKRs (execute quarterly)
