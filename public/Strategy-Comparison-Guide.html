⚖️ Which Strategy Framework?

Porter's vs Blue Ocean vs Playing to Win vs Wardley — when to use each and how they connect.

The Key Difference

These four frameworks form a strategic pipeline. They answer different questions at different stages. Porter's diagnoses. Blue Ocean discovers. Playing to Win decides. Wardley plans evolution.

⚔️
Porter's 5 Forces
Diagnose: How attractive is this market?
TIME: 2-4 hours
🌊
Blue Ocean Strategy
Discover: Where is uncontested space?
TIME: 1-2 weeks
🎮
Playing to Win
Decide: What integrated choices will we make?
TIME: 1-4 weeks
🗺️
Wardley Mapping
Plan: What's evolving and where to invest?
TIME: 2-8 hours per map

💡 One Sentence Each

Porter's: "Here's why this market is tough." Blue Ocean: "Here's where nobody's competing." Playing to Win: "Here are our specific choices." Wardley: "Here's what's changing and how to position."

🔗 The Strategic Pipeline

The real power is using them together. Each framework's output feeds the next one's input.

⚔️
Step 1: Porter's 5 Forces
Diagnose: "The music learning market has low barriers, high rivalry, and YouTube is a massive substitute threat."
⬇️
🌊
Step 2: Blue Ocean (ERRC)
Discover: "Eliminate song library arms race. Create AI teacher. Raise learning outcomes. Reduce gamification."
⬇️
🎮
Step 3: Playing to Win
Decide: "Play in adult beginners via mobile. Win through learning quality. Build capabilities in audio ML and pedagogy."
⬇️
🗺️
Step 4: Wardley Map
Plan: "AI audio = genesis (build). Gamification = product (adopt). Payments = commodity (outsource). AI will commoditize in 2-3 years — invest in next frontier now."

🎯 For PM Interviews

You rarely use all four in an interview. But naming the pipeline shows strategic maturity: "I'd start with a Porter's analysis of the competitive landscape, identify blue ocean opportunities using ERRC, then define specific strategic choices using a Playing to Win cascade. For execution, I'd map the value chain to determine build vs buy decisions." That sentence alone puts you ahead of 90% of PM candidates.

🔄 Cross-Framework Connections

Porter's "Substitute Threat" → Blue Ocean's "Non-Customers". The substitutes users choose instead of your product reveal the jobs they're actually trying to do.
Blue Ocean "Create" → Wardley's "Genesis". What you Create in ERRC maps to Genesis components in Wardley — these are where your competitive advantage lives.
PTW "Capabilities" → Wardley's "Custom-Built". The capabilities you choose to build should be the custom/genesis components on your map. If a "capability" is a commodity, rethink.
Blue Ocean "Eliminate" → Wardley's "Commodity". Things you eliminate in ERRC are often components that have already commoditized — the map validates the cut.
