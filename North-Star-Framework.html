The Core Idea

Why one metric rules them all

The Problem: Metric Overload

Most teams track dozens of metrics. DAU, MAU, revenue, NPS, conversion rate, session time, churn, activation rate... When everything is important, nothing is important. Teams optimize in different directions. Engineering improves page speed. Marketing maximizes signups. Product chases engagement. Nobody is aligned.

The North Star Metric fixes this: one number that captures the value you deliver to customers. When this number goes up, your business is healthy. When it goes down, you have a problem. Everyone knows it, everyone moves it.

❌ Bad: Revenue as North Star
Revenue is an output. You can increase revenue short-term by raising prices or cutting costs — without delivering more value. Revenue doesn't tell you if customers are happy.
✅ Good: "Lessons Completed" as North Star
Captures value delivered: a completed lesson = learning happened. If this goes up, retention improves, users are happy, and revenue follows. It's a leading indicator of business health.

🧠 Memory Trick

Like the actual North Star — sailors didn't need 50 stars to navigate. They needed one bright, fixed point to orient everything else. Your NSM is the one point the whole team navigates by.

🔬

Anatomy of a North Star

The metric + its input constellation

A Good North Star Metric Must Be:

💰
1. Connected to Revenue
When NSM goes up, revenue eventually follows. If there's no connection to business outcomes, it's a vanity metric.
❤️
2. Reflects Customer Value
Measures value delivered to the user, not extracted from them. "Lessons completed" (value delivered) not "subscription revenue" (value extracted).
📏
3. Measurable
You can track it weekly. "User happiness" isn't measurable. "Weekly active learners who complete 3+ lessons" is.
🎯
4. Actionable via Inputs
Teams can influence it through 3-5 input metrics they directly control. If you can't decompose it into levers, you can't move it.

🚩 Common NSM Traps

"Revenue" or "MRR": Output metric, not a value indicator. You can raise prices without delivering more value.
"Total signups": Vanity metric. 1M signups with 5% retention means 950K people who left.
"NPS": Lagging, survey-based, manipulable. Not measurable weekly.
"DAU" (for most companies): Too vague unless engagement IS your product (social media). For a learning app, a user who opens and closes isn't getting value.

Finding Your North Star

The 3-step method

Step-by-Step

Step 1: Define the Core Value
Ask: "What moment makes a user say 'this is worth it'?" For Skoove: playing a recognizable song. For Uber: arriving at your destination. For Notion: organizing a project. This is the value event.
Step 2: Make It Measurable
Turn the value event into a countable metric with a time window. "Songs completed per week" not just "songs completed." The time window creates urgency and enables trend tracking.
Step 3: Identify 3-5 Input Metrics
What levers can teams pull to move the North Star? These become each team's focus areas. Engineering might own "app load time." Content might own "lessons available." Growth owns "new activated users."

🔍 The Litmus Test

Run your candidate NSM through these 4 checks:

✅ Value test: If this metric doubles, are users genuinely getting more value?
✅ Revenue test: If this metric doubles, will revenue eventually follow?
✅ Game test: Is it hard to game? (Easy to game = "total pageviews." Hard to game = "lessons completed.")
✅ Lever test: Can you decompose it into 3-5 inputs that teams directly control?

💡 Pro Tip

Your NSM will likely change as your product matures. Early-stage: focus on activation ("users who complete onboarding"). Growth-stage: focus on engagement ("weekly active learners"). Mature: focus on retention or monetization. Re-evaluate every 6-12 months.

Real Example

North Star for Skoove / Trala

🎹 Skoove's North Star

⭐
Weekly Active Learners
Users who complete ≥3 lessons per week

Why this works: Completing 3+ lessons/week means the user is learning (value delivered), building a habit (retention predictor), and progressing (engagement). It passes all 4 tests: value (yes, learning), revenue (retained learners convert), gameable (no, requires real engagement), decomposable (yes, see inputs below).

Input Metrics (the levers):
👥
New Activated Users / week
Users who complete their first lesson. Owner: Growth team
📚
Lessons Available (quality-reviewed)
More good content = more reasons to come back. Owner: Content team
🔄
D7 Retention Rate
% of users who return 7 days after first use. Owner: Product team
⏱️
Avg. Lesson Completion Rate
% of started lessons that are finished. Owner: UX/Product team

🔗 How the Inputs Connect

More activated users (Input 1) who find quality content (Input 2) and come back after a week (Input 3) and finish what they start (Input 4) = more weekly active learners (North Star). Each team has a clear lever to pull. No confusion, no misalignment.

When to Use

Right situations and wrong ones

✅ Use North Star When...

  • ✔️ Aligning cross-functional teams on one goal
  • ✔️ Setting OKRs and quarterly goals
  • ✔️ PM interview asks "how would you measure success?"
  • ✔️ Building a product analytics dashboard
  • ✔️ Annual planning — what's the one number we rally around?

⛔ Skip North Star When...

  • ❌ Pre-product-market-fit (too early, the metric will change)
  • ❌ You need to diagnose WHERE growth is stuck (use AARRR)
  • ❌ Feature prioritization (use RICE/ICE instead)
  • ❌ Multiple products with different value props (need one NSM per product)
  • ❌ Short-term project decisions (NSM is long-term)
