📋 Executive Summary

Distill complex information into a crisp one-pager for leaders. The document that gets read first and sometimes only.

⚡

One-liner: An Executive Summary is a one-page document that gives leaders everything they need to make a decision: the recommendation, key data, risks, and ask. Written last but read first. It's the Pyramid Principle in document form.

One page. Six sections. Everything a leader needs to decide.
💡

Why One-Pagers?

How leaders actually read

The Reality of Executive Attention

Executives have 50+ decisions per week. They don't read 10-page docs. They scan the first page. If it's clear and compelling, they approve or ask questions. If it's unclear, they skip it or ask you to redo it.

The Executive Summary is the most important page of any document. It's written last (after you've done all the analysis) but read first (and sometimes only). Your 50-page research becomes a 1-page decision tool.

❌ The Info Dump
10-page doc. Conclusion on page 8. No clear recommendation. Leader has to read everything to understand the ask. Gets deprioritized.
✅ The Executive Summary
1 page. Recommendation in first sentence. Key data. Options. Risks. Clear ask. Leader decides in 3 minutes. Full analysis is an appendix if they want depth.

🧠 The Amazon Approach

Amazon famously uses 6-page memos read in silence at the start of meetings. Even those begin with the executive summary. Jeff Bezos: "If the summary is good, the details will follow." The discipline of compressing your thinking to one page forces clarity.

📋

The 6 Sections

Anatomy of a one-pager

1. Recommendation (2-3 sentences)
What you're proposing and why. First paragraph, first sentence. "I recommend we invest in Practice Mode for Trala to improve D30 retention from 8% to 16%." If they read nothing else, they have the answer.
2. Context (3-4 sentences)
Why this matters now. The problem or opportunity. Keep it brief — just enough for someone who hasn't been following the project to understand. Use SCQA format from Pyramid Principle.
3. Key Data (3-5 data points)
The evidence that supports your recommendation. Not all data — the DECISIVE data. "D30 retention: 8% (benchmark: 16%). 70% drop-off after lesson completion. Beta test: 3x practice frequency with Practice Mode."
4. Options Considered (2-3 options)
Show you evaluated alternatives. "Option A: Full practice suite (12 weeks, €180K). Option B: MVP practice mode (6 weeks, €80K) ← recommended. Option C: Do nothing (lose €84K/month in churn)." Leaders want to see you've thought broadly.
5. Risks & Mitigations (2-3 risks)
What could go wrong and how you'd handle it. "Risk: Audio engine can't handle tempo changes. Mitigation: Prototype tested with existing engine; fallback is fixed-speed practice." Acknowledging risks builds trust.
6. Ask / Next Steps (1-2 sentences)
What you need from the reader. Be specific. "Requesting: 1 designer + 2 engineers for 6-week cycle starting March 15. Decision needed by March 8." Never leave the ask ambiguous.
✍️

Writing Tips

What separates good from great

✅ Write it LAST, read it FIRST
Do all your analysis first. Then compress into 1 page. The exec summary is the distillation, not the starting point. You can't summarize what you haven't fully thought through.
✅ One page means ONE PAGE
If it spills to page 2, you haven't compressed enough. Cut context (they know more than you think). Cut redundant data. Remove qualifiers. Every sentence must earn its space.
✅ Use the "So What?" test
For every sentence, ask: "So what? Why does the reader care?" If a data point doesn't drive toward your recommendation, cut it. "Our NPS is 42" → so what? "Our NPS dropped from 58 to 42 after the redesign" → that matters.
✅ Bold the scannable path
A reader scanning your one-pager should get the story from just the bold text. Bold: recommendation, key numbers, option names, the ask. Reading only the bold should take 30 seconds and convey the core message.
❌ Common Mistakes
Burying the recommendation — it goes FIRST, not after context.
Including ALL data — only the 3-5 points that drive the decision.
No clear ask — "thoughts?" is not an ask. "Approve €80K budget by March 8" is.
Hedging language — "We might want to consider..." → "I recommend we..."

Real Example

Trala Practice Mode one-pager

📋 EXECUTIVE SUMMARY: Trala Practice Mode
Recommendation: Launch Practice Mode MVP (tempo control, section looping, mistake heatmaps) in a 6-week cycle to improve D30 retention from 8% to target 16%. Estimated impact: €84K/month recovered in reduced churn.
Context: Trala's D30 retention (8%) is half the music ed benchmark (16%). 70% of users who complete a lesson never practice it. The current "Practice" button just replays the lesson. Without structured practice, users don't build muscle memory and churn at week 3.
Key Data: D30 retention: 8% (benchmark: 16%). Lesson replay rate for L3-5: 4x higher than other lessons (users WANT to review). Beta test (n=50): practice frequency tripled, completion rate up 25%. Engineering estimate: 6 weeks, reuses existing audio engine.
Options: A) Full practice suite (12 wks, €180K) — too broad for first iteration. B) MVP practice mode (6 wks, €80K) ← recommended — validates concept, fastest to impact. C) Do nothing — €84K/month continues to churn.
Risks: 1) Tempo changes may cause audio sync issues → prototype tested successfully with existing engine. 2) Practice adoption may be low → progressive onboarding + in-app prompts planned. 3) Metric may not move enough → built-in A/B test from day 1.
Ask: Approve 1 designer + 2 engineers for 6-week cycle starting March 15. Decision needed by March 8. Full analysis available in appendix doc.

💡 Why This Works

A CPO reads this in 3 minutes and has everything: the recommendation, the evidence, the options, the risks, and the specific ask. No need to read a 20-page doc. The appendix exists for anyone who wants to go deeper.

When to Use

The doc that opens every door

✅ Use Exec Summary When...

  • ✔️ Product proposals needing leadership approval
  • ✔️ Quarterly business reviews
  • ✔️ PRDs (as the first page)
  • ✔️ Post-mortems and retrospective reports
  • ✔️ Any doc that will be shared upward in the org

⛔ Skip Exec Summary When...

  • ❌ Quick Slack updates (use Pyramid directly)
  • ❌ Interview answers (use STAR)
  • ❌ Presentations (use Narrative Arc)
  • ❌ Internal team docs that don't go to leadership
  • ❌ Brainstorming or exploration docs

🔗 Connections

Pyramid
Exec Summary IS a Pyramid in document form. Recommendation = top. Sections = grouped reasons. Data = evidence.
STAR
Your STAR stories can become exec summaries for portfolio case studies. S+T = Context, A = Options + Recommendation, R = Key Data.
Narrative Arc
Present the Narrative Arc in the meeting, then follow up with the Exec Summary doc. Spoken story + written one-pager = full coverage.
