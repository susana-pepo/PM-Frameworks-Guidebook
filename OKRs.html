⚙️ Execution

🎯 OKRs

Objectives & Key Results: set ambitious goals (Objectives) and measure progress with concrete outcomes (Key Results). Used by Google, Intel, Spotify.

⚡

One-liner: Objective = inspiring, qualitative direction ("Where do we want to go?"). Key Results = measurable outcomes that prove you got there ("How do we know we arrived?"). Set quarterly. 70% achievement = success (stretch goals).

🎯 Objective: Make Skoove the #1 app for piano beginners
KR1: Increase WAL from 30K to 50K
KR2: Improve D30 retention from 8% to 15%
KR3: Reach 4.7⭐ App Store rating
Objective = inspirational direction. Key Results = measurable proof of progress.
💡

Why OKRs?

Alignment, focus, accountability

The Problem OKRs Solve

Without OKRs, teams set vague goals ("improve the product"), work on whatever seems urgent, and can't tell at the end of the quarter whether they succeeded. OKRs create focus (max 3-5 objectives), alignment (everyone sees the same goals), and accountability (measurable results you grade at quarter end).

Created by Andy Grove at Intel (1970s), popularized by John Doerr at Google (1999). Used by most top tech companies.

❌ Without OKRs
"Our goal is to grow." → Teams work on different things → No way to measure success → End of quarter: "Did we do well?" "🤷 Maybe?"
✅ With OKRs
"Objective: Delight new users in first week. KR: D7 retention from 20% to 35%." → Everyone aligned → End of Q: "D7 hit 29%. 0.6 score. Good progress, here's what we learned."

🧠 Memory Trick

Think of a road trip. The Objective is the destination ("Drive to the beach!"). Key Results are the mile markers that prove you're getting there ("Pass the city limits by 10am, reach the highway by 11am, arrive by 1pm"). Without mile markers, you don't know if you're on track until you either arrive or run out of gas.

OKR Anatomy

Rules for good objectives and key results

🎯 Objectives — The "What"

✅ Qualitative: Inspiring, not a number. "Delight new users" not "Get 50K users."
✅ Ambitious: Stretch goals that push the team. If you always hit 100%, you're not aiming high enough.
✅ Time-bound: One quarter (sometimes annual). Creates urgency.
✅ 3-5 max: More than 5 objectives = no focus. Fewer is better.

📊 Key Results — The "How We Know"

✅ Quantitative: Always a number. "From X to Y" format is gold. "Increase D7 retention from 20% to 35%."
✅ Outcomes, not outputs: "D7 retention hits 35%" (outcome) not "Ship 5 onboarding features" (output). Outputs are tasks, not results.
✅ 2-5 per objective: Enough to cover the objective, not so many you lose focus.
✅ Gradeable: At quarter end, score 0.0 to 1.0. 0.7 = success for stretch goals.

🚩 Common Mistakes

KRs as tasks: "Launch redesigned onboarding" ❌ That's a task. "Onboarding completion rate hits 70%" ✅ That's a result.
Too many OKRs: 8 objectives with 5 KRs each = 40 things to track. Nobody will remember them.
Sandbagging: Setting easy goals to look good. If you always score 1.0, you're not stretching.
Using OKRs for performance reviews: This kills ambitious goal-setting. People set safe goals to protect their bonus.

Writing Great OKRs

The formula and cascade

The Formula

I will [Objective] as measured by [Key Results]
This sentence forces you to connect the inspirational goal with measurable proof. If you can't fill in both blanks, your OKR needs work.

🏢 The Cascade: Company → Team → Individual

Company OKR
O: Become the market leader in music education apps
KR: Reach 1M MAU | KR: Hit €2M ARR | KR: Achieve #1 in App Store Education category
⬇️ cascades to
Product Team OKR
O: Delight new users in their first week
KR: D7 retention from 20% to 35% | KR: First-lesson completion from 40% to 70% | KR: NPS for new users from 30 to 50
⬇️ cascades to
Content Team OKR (your team)
O: Create an irresistible first-week learning journey
KR: Publish 5 beginner-optimized "quick win" lessons | KR: Lesson completion rate for Week 1 content reaches 80% | KR: "Song I know" available within first 3 lessons
Each level's KRs feed the level above. Content team's work drives Product team's retention, which drives Company's MAU.

💡 Committed vs Aspirational

Committed OKRs (also called "rooftop" OKRs): Must hit 1.0. These are promises. "Ship GDPR compliance by March 31." Aspirational OKRs (also called "moonshot"): Expect 0.6-0.7. These push teams beyond comfort. "Achieve #1 App Store ranking." Label each OKR so everyone knows which type it is.

Q2 OKRs for Skoove Content Team

🎹 Susana's Team — Q2 2026 OKRs

Objective 1: Make every beginner feel like a musician in Week 1
KR1: First-lesson completion rate: 40% → 70%Aspirational

🔗 How This Connects to Other Frameworks

North Star (Weekly Active Learners) = the shared output everyone contributes to. Input/Output: KR3 of Obj 1 is an input metric (lessons published). KR1 of Obj 1 is an output metric (completion rate). Sprints: Each KR breaks into sprint-level tasks — "This sprint we build the quick-win lesson template."

When to Use

Right situations and pitfalls

✅ Use OKRs When...

  • ✔️ Quarterly goal-setting and alignment
  • ✔️ Multiple teams need to coordinate toward shared goals
  • ✔️ PM interview asks about goal-setting or measuring success
  • ✔️ Company needs focus — doing too many things at once
  • ✔️ Translating strategy into measurable quarterly actions

⛔ Be Careful When...

  • ❌ Pre-PMF startups (goals change too fast for quarterly cycles)
  • ❌ Tying OKRs to compensation (kills ambitious goal-setting)
  • ❌ Using KRs as task lists ("Ship feature X" is a task, not a result)
  • ❌ Setting 10+ objectives (defeats the purpose of focus)
  • ❌ Never reviewing mid-quarter (OKRs need check-ins)

🔗 Connections

North Star
North Star = Objective. Input metrics = Key Results. Direct translation.
Input/Output
Outputs = Objectives. Inputs = Key Results. Counter-metrics = guardrails on KRs.
Scrum
OKRs set quarterly direction. Sprints execute toward those goals, 2 weeks at a time.
RICE
RICE prioritizes the backlog. OKRs determine WHICH backlog items align with quarterly goals.
