Agile vs Scrum

The philosophy and the framework

Agile = Mindset. Scrum = Method.

Agile is a philosophy: build iteratively, respond to change, prioritize working software over documentation, collaborate with customers. It's captured in the Agile Manifesto (2001).

Scrum is the most popular implementation of Agile. It gives you specific roles (PO, Scrum Master, Dev Team), ceremonies (standup, planning, review, retro), and artifacts (backlog, sprint backlog, increment). Think of Agile as "eating healthy" and Scrum as "a specific meal plan."

❌ Waterfall (Before Agile)
Plan everything upfront → Design → Build all of it → Test → Launch. Takes months. If requirements change? Start over. Learning happens at the END.
✅ Agile/Scrum
Plan a little → Build a little → Ship it → Get feedback → Adapt. Every 2 weeks. Learning happens EVERY sprint. Requirements can change between sprints.

🧠 The 4 Agile Values

Individuals & interactions over processes & tools. Working software over comprehensive documentation. Customer collaboration over contract negotiation. Responding to change over following a plan. The items on the right have value, but we value the items on the left more.

Scrum Roles & Ceremonies

The people and the rituals

👥 The 3 Roles

👑
Product Owner (PO)
Owns the what and why. Maintains the product backlog, prioritizes work, writes user stories, represents the customer. Decides what to build and in what order. This is you (or your future role).
🛡️
Scrum Master
Owns the process. Removes blockers, facilitates ceremonies, coaches the team on Scrum practices, protects the team from interruptions. NOT a project manager — a servant-leader.
💻
Development Team
Owns the how. Cross-functional (devs, designers, QA). Self-organizing — they decide HOW to deliver the sprint goals. Usually 3-9 people.

📅 The 5 Ceremonies

📋 Backlog Refinement
PO + team review upcoming stories, clarify requirements, estimate effort. Ongoing, ~1h/week. Makes planning smoother.
🎯 Sprint Planning
Start of sprint. PO presents priorities, team selects what they can deliver. Output: sprint goal + sprint backlog. ~2h for a 2-week sprint.
☀️ Daily Standup
15 min, every day. Each person: What I did yesterday, what I'll do today, any blockers. NOT a status report to the manager — it's team coordination.
🚀 Sprint Review
End of sprint. Team demos working software to stakeholders. Get feedback. PO accepts/rejects work. ~1h.
🔍 Retrospective
After review. Team only. What went well? What didn't? What to improve? Commit to 1-2 action items. The continuous improvement engine. ~1h.

The Sprint Cycle

Artifacts and flow

The 3 Artifacts

📋 Product Backlog
The master list of everything to build, owned by PO. Ordered by priority. Items near the top are refined and ready; bottom items are vague and far out. It's a living document — always changing.
🎯 Sprint Backlog
Subset of the product backlog selected for THIS sprint + a plan. Owned by the dev team. Once sprint starts, no new items added (protect the sprint). If scope must change, renegotiate with PO.
🚀 Increment
The working, shippable product at the end of the sprint. Must meet the "Definition of Done" (DoD). Each sprint adds to the previous increments. The increment is what you demo at sprint review.

📝 User Stories

The standard format for backlog items. Written from the user's perspective:

As a [user type], I want to [action] so that [benefit].
Example: "As a piano beginner, I want to see my progress after each lesson so that I feel motivated to continue practicing."

Acceptance criteria: Clear, testable conditions that define "done" for this story. "Progress bar updates after lesson completion. Shows % complete for current course. Includes streak counter."

📊 Estimation: Story Points

Teams estimate effort using relative sizing (story points on Fibonacci: 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13). A "5" is roughly twice as complex as a "3." Points measure complexity + uncertainty, not hours.

1-2: Small, clear
3-5: Medium, some unknowns
8: Large, risky
13+: Too big, break it down

💡 Velocity

Velocity = total story points completed per sprint. After 3-5 sprints, you have a reliable average. This helps predict how much the team can deliver in future sprints. Velocity is for planning, NOT for performance reviews. Using it as a productivity metric destroys trust.

Real Example

A Trala sprint in action

🎻 Sprint 14: "Improve First-Session Experience"

🎯 Sprint Goal
Reduce time-to-first-note from 4 minutes to under 90 seconds for new Trala users.
📋 Sprint Backlog (28 points)
Skip instrument setup for first lesson5 pts
Inline tuning check (no separate screen)8 pts
Auto-play demo of first exercise5 pts
"Your first note!" celebration animation3 pts
Analytics: track time-to-first-note3 pts
Bug: audio latency on Android 12+4 pts
🚀 Sprint Review Outcome
Demoed to stakeholders. Time-to-first-note reduced from 4 min to 1:15. Android bug fixed. Celebration animation got great feedback from Head of Design. 26/28 points completed (inline tuning partially done, carries to Sprint 15).
🔍 Retro Highlights
Good: Clear sprint goal kept everyone focused. Pairing on the tuning feature unblocked progress. Improve: QA started too late — should start testing Day 3, not Day 8. Action: Add "QA handoff" milestone to mid-sprint.

When to Use

Right situations and alternatives

✅ Use Scrum When...

  • ✔️ Cross-functional team of 3-9 people
  • ✔️ Requirements are evolving or uncertain
  • ✔️ You need regular stakeholder feedback
  • ✔️ Team needs structure but also flexibility
  • ✔️ Most software product teams

⛔ Consider Alternatives When...

  • ❌ Team < 3 people (too much ceremony overhead)
  • ❌ Work is mostly support/maintenance (use Kanban)
  • ❌ Projects need 6+ weeks of focused work (see Shape Up)
  • ❌ You need parallel discovery + delivery (see Dual Track)
  • ❌ Hardware or regulated industries (Scrum alone may not fit)

🔗 Connections

Shape Up
Scrum = fixed sprint, flexible scope. Shape Up = fixed scope, flexible schedule (6 weeks). Different tradeoff.
Dual Track
Dual Track adds a discovery track alongside Scrum's delivery track. Research and build in parallel.
OKRs
OKRs set quarterly goals. Sprints are how you execute toward those goals, 2 weeks at a time.
RICE
Use RICE to prioritize the product backlog. Then pull top items into each sprint.
