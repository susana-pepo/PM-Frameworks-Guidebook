🧩

The 4 Quadrants

Each one tells you exactly what to do

🏆

Quick Wins

High Value · Low Effort

The gold. These features deliver big impact for little work. Do these first — they build momentum and show fast results to stakeholders.

→ Action: Build immediately. These are your sprint priorities.
🎯

Big Bets

High Value · High Effort

Worth doing but expensive. These need careful planning — break them into phases, validate assumptions first, and staff them well.

→ Action: Plan & phase. Put on the roadmap but don't rush.
🤷

Fill-Ins

Low Value · Low Effort

Nice-to-haves. Easy to build but won't move the needle. Good for filling gaps between big projects or for hackathons.

→ Action: Do only if you have spare capacity. Never prioritize these.
🗑️

Avoid

Low Value · High Effort

The trap. These look important because they're complex, but they eat resources and deliver little. Kill them early.

→ Action: Say no. Remove from backlog or revisit assumptions.

🧠 Memory trick

Read the grid like a priority list: 🏆 Quick Wins → 🎯 Big Bets → 🤷 Fill-Ins → 🗑️ Avoid. Top-left is best, bottom-right is worst. Always start top-left.

📐

How to Score

Rate Value and Effort on simple scales

V
Value — "How much does this matter?"

Combine user impact, business impact, and strategic alignment into one gut-check score.

1MinimalNice but nobody asked
2LowSome users care
3MediumClear improvement
4HighMoves a key metric
5CriticalGame-changer or blocker
Ask: "If we DON'T build this, what happens?" If the answer is "nothing much" → it's a 1-2.

E
Effort — "How hard is this to build?"

Include design, engineering, QA, and any cross-team dependencies.

1TinyA few hours
2Small1–2 days
3Medium1–2 weeks
4LargeWeeks to a month
5MassiveMonths, multi-team
Low effort is GOOD here (unlike Value where high is good). That's why Quick Wins sit in the top-left.

⚖️ Key difference from RICE

RICE uses precise numbers (Reach = 10,000 users). Value vs Effort uses relative 1–5 scores. It's faster but less rigorous — perfect for quick alignment, not for detailed quarterly planning.

When to Use

Fast and visual, but not always the right tool

✅ Use this when...

  • You need to align a team in 30 minutes or less
  • Backlog grooming — quick sort of 5–20 items
  • Stakeholders want a visual they can understand instantly
  • Early-stage product with limited data
  • You need a "first pass" before deeper analysis (like RICE)

🚫 Skip this when...

  • You need precise, data-backed rankings → use RICE
  • Comparing more than 20 features (grid gets crowded)
  • Decisions need to hold up to exec scrutiny
  • Effort estimates vary wildly across team members
  • You're comparing fundamentally different types of work

🔗 How it connects to RICE

Use Value vs Effort as a fast filter to narrow 30 ideas down to 10 strong candidates. Then use RICE to rigorously rank those 10. The two frameworks are partners, not competitors.

⚠️

5 Common Pitfalls

Simple framework, subtle traps

1

Everything lands in Quick Wins

If 80% of features are "high value, low effort," your scoring isn't honest. You're either inflating value or underestimating effort.

Force distribution: aim for roughly equal items per quadrant. If a quadrant is empty, re-calibrate.
2

Confusing "Value" with "Cool"

A feature can be exciting and innovative but deliver zero business or user value. "Cool" ≠ "valuable."

Define Value before scoring: user impact + revenue impact + strategic alignment. Score against those, not excitement.
3

Only counting engineering effort

Design, QA, docs, data migration, training, support — all of these are effort. Ignoring them makes everything look cheaper than it is.

Ask: "Who needs to do work for this to ship?" Count ALL of those people's time.
4

HiPPO anchoring

The Highest-Paid Person's Opinion goes first and everyone agrees. Classic anchoring bias.

Silent scoring first. Everyone writes their V and E on a sticky note, then reveal simultaneously.
5

Using it for detailed planning

Value vs Effort is a sorting tool, not a planning tool. It tells you what to focus on, not how to build it or exactly when.

Use it to decide WHAT, then switch to RICE or roadmapping to decide WHEN and HOW.

Run a Session

45-minute ready-to-use agenda

👥 Who's in the room?

Must have

PM (facilitator), Engineering lead, Design lead

Nice to have

Stakeholder, Data analyst, anyone who'll build the work

🧰 What to prepare

A whiteboard or Miro/FigJam with a 2×2 grid drawn. Sticky notes (physical or digital). Pre-written list of 8–15 candidate features — one per sticky. Don't bring more than 20 or the session drags.

0–5

Set context

Explain the matrix. Define what "Value" means for THIS session (user impact? revenue? retention?). Agree on effort scope.

5–15

Silent scoring

Everyone independently scores each feature: V (1–5) and E (1–5) on their own sticky notes. No discussion yet.

15–25

Reveal & plot

Go feature by feature. Everyone reveals scores. Average or discuss outliers. Place the sticky on the grid.

25–35

Debate the edges

Focus on features near quadrant borders — these are the ones where placement matters. Timebox: 2 min per feature.

35–45

Decide & commit

Quick Wins → next sprint. Big Bets → roadmap slot. Fill-Ins → backlog. Avoid → archive or kill. Take a photo of the board.

💡 Facilitation tips

Define "Value" upfront — if you don't, everyone scores against different criteria and the grid is meaningless.

Focus debates on the edges — items clearly in a quadrant don't need discussion. Items ON the border do.

Photo the result — snap the whiteboard/Miro. You'll reference this for weeks.

Quick Quiz

5 questions to lock it in

1. A feature with High Value and Low Effort goes in which quadrant?

Quick Wins = high value, low effort. Build these first — they're your best ROI.

2. Your team scored 12 out of 15 features as "Quick Wins." What's the problem?

If everything is a Quick Win, you're inflating Value or underestimating Effort. Force a more honest distribution.

3. What should you do with "Big Bet" items?

Big Bets are worth doing but need planning. Break into phases, validate assumptions, staff well.

4. What's the best way to prevent HiPPO bias in a scoring session?

Silent scoring prevents anchoring. Everyone commits to their V and E independently, then reveals together.

5. Value vs Effort is best used as...

Use it to quickly sort 30 ideas down to 10, then RICE to rank those 10 rigorously. They're partners.
